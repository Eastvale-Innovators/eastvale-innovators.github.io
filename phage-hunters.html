The Hunt For Something New

Bacteriophages — viruses that prey on bacteria — outnumber every other biological entity on the planet. Phage Hunters was our attempt to find one that had never been catalogued.

Why Phages?

With antibiotic resistance rising, phages are being rediscovered as precision weapons against stubborn bacterial infections. Every new phage is a potential tool — and nearly every soil sample on Earth hides ones science has never seen.

We wanted to join that global search from our own backyard.

The Ambition

Our goal was simple to state and brutal to execute: collect, isolate, purify, characterize, and sequence a novel phage against a safe host bacterium — then document it well enough that someone, somewhere, could use it.

We didn't get all the way there. But we got further than we had any right to.

The Methodology

A real phage hunt isn't one experiment — it's a pipeline. Each step narrows a chaotic soil sample into a single, purified virus you can study.

Soil Sampling

Collect soil from varied microhabitats — compost, root systems, standing water — where bacterial populations (and their predators) thrive.

Enrichment

Incubate the sample with the target host bacterium so any lurking phages can multiply into detectable numbers.

Plaque Assay

Spread on a bacterial lawn. Clear circular "plaques" reveal spots where phages have lysed the cells.

Purification

Pick a single plaque and re-plate, repeating until the culture is confidently one phage strain — not a mixed population.

High-Titer Lysate

Scale the isolated phage up to concentrations high enough to image, sequence, and characterize.

Electron Imaging

Use TEM (transmission electron microscopy) — where available — to classify morphology: siphovirus, myovirus, podovirus.

DNA Extraction

Lyse the capsid, purify the genome, quantify, and prep for sequencing.

Sequencing & Annotation

Map the genome, identify genes, and compare against phage databases. If nothing matches closely — it might be new.

Field Log

Abbreviated entries from the project's working notebook.

// ENTRY 01 · PROJECT KICKOFF

Mission brief

Crew assembled. Target host selected. Collection routes plotted across local green spaces. Optimism at 100%.

// ENTRY 02 · FIRST SAMPLES

Soil acquired, samples logged

Samples from six sites. GPS, depth, moisture, and surrounding flora recorded. Buffer-extracted and stored cold.

// ENTRY 03 · FIRST PLAQUES

Plaques observed

Three of six enrichments produced plaques on the bacterial lawn. Morphology varied — clear, turbid, bullseye. Promising candidates flagged.

// ENTRY 04 · PURIFICATION LOOPS

Single-plaque purification

Three rounds of re-plating converged on two apparently clonal phages. Stability checked across passages.

// ENTRY 05 · ROADBLOCK

Equipment access stalled

Access to sequencing and electron microscopy hit a wall. Timeline slipped. Morale adjusted accordingly.

// ENTRY 06 · MISSION ARCHIVED

Project paused, knowledge preserved

With core members graduating and lab access unresolved, the hunt was formally archived. Protocols, raw data, and field notes were packaged for any future team that wants to pick up the trail.

Lessons From The Lab Bench

We underestimated infrastructure. Hunting a phage is mostly cheap — Petri dishes, top agar, patience — until it isn't. The moment you need a sequencer or an electron microscope, you are entirely at the mercy of someone else's calendar.

If anyone picks this up again: secure sequencing access before the first sample is ever collected. Everything else, we can teach you.

— Phage Hunters Crew, closing log
